What is a CTF?

Capture The Flag Explained

Capture The Flag (CTF) competitions are cybersecurity contests where participants solve security challenges to find hidden "flags" — usually a text string like FLAG{s0me_s3cr3t_string}. They're completely legal, educational, and the best way to build practical skills.

CTF Challenge Categories

🌐 Web
SQLi, XSS, SSRF, authentication bypass, cookie manipulation
💥 Pwn / Binary
Buffer overflows, heap exploits, format strings, ROP chains
🔑 Cryptography
RSA, AES, hashing, encoding, classical ciphers, custom crypto
🔬 Forensics
File analysis, steganography, pcap analysis, memory dumps
🔭 Reversing
Disassemble binaries, decompile code, understand algorithms
🕵️ OSINT
Open source intelligence, finding hidden information online

🏆 CTF Competition Formats

  • Jeopardy: Independent challenges worth points. Most common format. Solve any category in any order.
  • Attack/Defence: Teams attack opponents' services while defending their own. Requires team coordination.
  • King of the Hill: Take control of a server and hold it against other teams.
  • Mixed: Combination of Jeopardy and A/D formats. Used in major competitions.

✅ Why CTFs Are Essential

  • Legal, safe environment to practice offensive techniques
  • Directly builds skills tested in certifications like OSCP
  • Impressive on resumes — ranked players get recruited
  • Great for finding security community and teammates
  • Teaches problem-solving and research skills

Strategy

How to Succeed at CTFs

🔰

For Beginners

  • Start with PicoCTF — persistent challenges, great for learning
  • Don't skip categories — web and forensics are most beginner-accessible
  • Read write-ups immediately after getting stuck for 30 mins
  • Keep notes on every technique you encounter
  • Join TryHackMe — guided rooms teach the skills CTFs test
  • Progress is not linear — some challenges click instantly, others don't
⚙️

Essential CTF Toolkit

  • Kali Linux — your base platform
  • Burp Suite — all web challenges
  • Python 3 — scripting solutions and crypto
  • Ghidra / x64dbg — reverse engineering
  • Wireshark — network and forensics
  • CyberChef — encoding/decoding everything
  • pwntools — binary exploitation scripting
  • Steghide / exiftool — steganography
📝

Write-Up Culture

  • Always write up challenges you solved — even easy ones
  • Publish on a blog, GitHub, or Medium for visibility
  • Read other team's write-ups after events — learn different approaches
  • CTFtime.org archives write-ups from all major events
  • A strong write-up portfolio is impressive to security employers
  • Write-ups teach as much as solving the challenge itself

Community

Building or Joining a CTF Team

🤝 Finding a Team

  • Check CTFtime.org — teams recruit members publicly
  • Join TryHackMe or HTB Discord — team-finding channels exist
  • Local DEF CON groups often have CTF teams
  • University security clubs always welcome members
  • Perform well solo first — good players get recruited
  • Starting your own small team is legitimate — even 2–3 people helps

🎯 Team Specialisation Strategy

Strong CTF teams have specialists in different categories, not generalists:

  • Assign web, pwn, RE, crypto, and forensics specialists
  • Designate a team captain who coordinates and prioritises challenges
  • Use collaborative notes tools (HackMD, Notion) during competitions
  • Share flag submission duties — avoid duplication of work
  • Post-competition reviews are as valuable as the competition itself
